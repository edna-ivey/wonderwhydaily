A lightning bolt looks as if someone drew across the sky with a shaky glowing pencil. That crooked path is not random decoration. It is the record of electricity searching for a route.

Inside a storm cloud, collisions among ice, water, and rising air separate electrical charges. When the difference becomes strong enough, a faint channel of charged air begins moving outward. This channel, called a stepped leader, does not travel in one smooth leap. It advances in short bursts, pauses, branches, and tries again.

Air is not identical everywhere. Moisture, dust, temperature, and existing electrical charge make some small regions easier to cross than others. The leader explores those possibilities like roots spreading through soil. Many branches stop. One may eventually connect with an opposite charge reaching up from the ground or another part of the cloud.

Once that connection forms, a powerful return stroke races along the completed path. That is the brilliant flash you notice most clearly. It lights up the successful route, including all its turns.

Lightning does not know its destination in advance. Its zigzag is the visible trail of a high-speed search, with the sky testing many doors before electricity finds one it can burst through.
