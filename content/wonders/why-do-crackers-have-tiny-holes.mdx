The tiny holes in a cracker may look decorative, but they are actually escape hatches. Without them, an ordinary flat cracker could enter the oven and begin inflating like a collection of little balloons.

Cracker dough contains water. As the dough heats, some of that water becomes steam. If steam gets trapped between layers or beneath a drying surface, it pushes the dough upward and makes bubbles. That is wonderful when a baker wants a flaky pocket or a dramatic puff. It is less helpful when the goal is a thin, evenly crisp cracker.

Before baking, machines or tools punch holes through the dough in a process called docking. The holes give steam easier paths out. They also help heat move through the cracker more evenly, so the center can dry and crisp without the surface burning first.

Different crackers use different patterns because recipes behave differently. A bubbly artisan cracker may have only a few holes. A very flat snack cracker may be covered with them. The pattern can even become part of the cracker's familiar appearance.

Those dots are therefore not missing pieces. They are tiny bits of baking engineering, placed exactly where the dough needs room to breathe.
