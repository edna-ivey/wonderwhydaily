A cave large enough to swallow buildings can begin with cracks barely wide enough for water. The transformation happens because water is patient, gravity keeps it moving, and some rocks can slowly dissolve.

Rainwater absorbs carbon dioxide from the air and soil, forming a weak acid. When that water seeps into limestone, it reacts with the rock and carries tiny amounts away. At first, the water follows narrow joints and fractures. As those openings widen, more water can pass through, speeding the growth of underground channels.

Over long periods, connected passages can become tunnels, chambers, and underground rivers. If the water table drops, some spaces drain and fill with air. Mineral-rich drips may then build stalactites from ceilings and stalagmites from floors, decorating a chamber that was already carved by water.

Not every cave forms this way. Lava can leave tubes behind. Waves can carve sea caves. Ice and other processes create still more varieties. But many of the world's enormous cave systems grew through the slow dissolving work of groundwater in soluble rock.

A giant cave is therefore not simply an empty place beneath the ground. It is the shape left behind by water traveling the same hidden routes again and again for longer than a human mind can easily picture.
