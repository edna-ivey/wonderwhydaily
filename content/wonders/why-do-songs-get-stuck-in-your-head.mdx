Sometimes a song stops playing in the room and keeps playing in the one place with no off switch: your head.

Catchy, familiar, or unfinished musical patterns are easy for the brain to replay. A short chorus can loop when attention is lightly occupied, even though no sound is entering your ears.

The loop often arrives during low-focus moments such as walking, showering, or doing chores. With no demanding task taking center stage, the brain has room to replay a musical fragment on its own. Repetition makes that mental path easier to revisit.

Earworms often focus on one fragment instead of the whole song. Listening through to the ending can sometimes help because it gives the repeating pattern a sense of completion.

An earworm is a reminder that the brain does not merely receive music. It can quietly perform music for itself.

The tune may be only a few seconds long, but familiarity lets your mind rebuild it each time the loop comes around.
