A shark does not treat a tooth like a treasured possession. It treats one more like a disposable blade on a constantly moving tool.

Human teeth are anchored firmly in sockets. Shark teeth sit in softer tissue and are arranged in multiple rows. The front row does the biting, while rows behind it wait at angles inside the jaw. When a working tooth breaks or falls out, a replacement slowly rolls forward, almost like a new product moving to the front of a store shelf.

That system is extremely useful when your meals can fight back. A shark may bite slippery fish, hard shells, bones, or rough skin. Losing a tooth during dinner is not a dental emergency; it is routine maintenance. Different shark species even carry differently shaped tools. Some have pointed teeth for gripping, while others have broad teeth for crushing.

The strangest part is that the whole jaw lining acts like a slow conveyor belt. New teeth develop farther back, shift toward the edge, take a turn at the front, and eventually disappear.

A shark's famous grin is never really finished. It is a living production line, continuously rebuilding itself one lost tooth at a time.
