Imagine meeting a bright red fruit from across the ocean while knowing that some of its plant relatives can make people seriously ill. Suspicion would not be entirely unreasonable.

Tomatoes belong to the nightshade family, which includes poisonous plants as well as familiar foods such as potatoes and eggplants. Their leaves and stems also contain compounds that should not be eaten. To someone already wary of nightshades, the unfamiliar fruit could look like a warning dressed in bright red.

When tomatoes arrived in Europe, many people did not immediately treat them as dinner. Gardeners valued the plants for their unusual appearance, and wealthy households sometimes grew them as ornamentals. A beautiful foreign plant could be admired without anyone trusting it enough to put on a plate.

Not everyone feared tomatoes, and attitudes changed differently across places and social classes. People in the Americas had cultivated and eaten tomatoes long before Europeans encountered them. As cooks elsewhere used the ripe fruit more often, suspicion slowly gave way to recipes.

Today tomatoes appear in sauces, salads, soups, and sandwiches around the world. Their journey from decorative curiosity to kitchen essential shows how unfamiliarity can make even an ordinary food seem dangerous. Sometimes the strangest ingredient is simply the one nobody at the table has learned to trust yet.
