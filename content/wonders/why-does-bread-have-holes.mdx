Slice into a loaf and you reveal a map of bubbles. Some are tiny and even; others form great tunnels. Each hole began as a pocket of gas inside the dough.

Yeast feeds on sugars and releases carbon dioxide. The gas would simply escape from a bowl of liquid, but bread dough contains gluten, a stretchy network of proteins created as flour mixes with water and is worked or rested.

Gluten wraps around growing gas bubbles, allowing the dough to inflate without immediately popping. During baking, heat makes gases expand and water turn to steam. The dough rises rapidly until heat sets the starches and proteins into a firm structure. The bubbles remain as holes after the gas escapes and the bread cools.

Different breads manage bubbles differently. Stiff dough and firm shaping can produce a close, even crumb. Wet dough, gentle handling, and long fermentation can encourage large, irregular openings.

The holes are not missing bread caused by careless baking. They are evidence that the dough was alive with fermentation and flexible enough to hold its breath. A loaf's crumb is a frozen record of countless bubbles growing, pressing together, and finally becoming architecture.
