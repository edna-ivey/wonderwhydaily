A cloud can become a dragon, a face, and a sandwich in under a minute without changing nearly as much as your imagination does.

Your brain constantly searches messy visual information for familiar patterns. Clouds have loose edges and no single correct interpretation, so your pattern-finding system can quickly turn them into faces, animals, or objects.

This pattern-finding habit is called pareidolia. It is not limited to vision: the same eager search for meaning can make people think they hear a word hidden inside a burst of fuzzy sound. Your brain would rather test a possible pattern than miss one.

Faces get special attention. Humans can spot one from only a few rough marks, which is why outlets, cars, and pieces of toast can suddenly seem to have expressions.

Your brain is such an enthusiastic pattern hunter that a few soft edges can become a whole cast of characters.

One person can spot a rabbit where another sees a ship, because the cloud supplies only hints and each brain completes a different picture.
