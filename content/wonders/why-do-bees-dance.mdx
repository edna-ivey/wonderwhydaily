A honeybee can find an excellent patch of flowers, fly home through the dark, and hand the colony a tiny treasure map without drawing a single line.

Back on the honeycomb, the forager performs a waggle dance. It loops around, then runs through the middle while wiggling its body. The angle of that straight waggle run tells other bees which direction to fly compared with the Sun. The length of the waggle helps communicate distance: a longer waggle usually means a longer journey.

The dance happens inside a crowded, dark hive, so the audience is not simply watching a performance. Nearby bees feel vibrations and follow the dancer closely with their bodies and antennae. Then they head outside, using the dance's directions and the flower scent carried home by the forager to finish the search.

A bee is basically carrying a tiny treasure map inside its dance. Even more wonderfully, that map keeps working while the Sun moves across the sky because bees can adjust their directions over time.

One successful forager does not need to lead everyone back personally. A few seconds of wiggling can send many workers toward the same patch of flowers. The colony turns movement into shared knowledge, and a dance into dinner.
