A bite of spicy salsa can make your mouth feel fiery and your nose behave as though you walked into a rainstorm. The pepper is not actually raising your mouth to a dangerous temperature. It is pressing a very convincing alarm button.

Chili peppers contain capsaicin. This molecule attaches to nerve receptors that normally help detect real heat and irritation. Your brain receives a message that sounds a lot like, “Something hot is happening here,” even when the food itself is room temperature.

Your body responds with several cooling and cleaning reactions. You may sweat, your eyes may water, and glands inside your nose may release extra mucus. That runny nose is an attempt to dilute and wash away the apparent irritant. The official name for this particular food-triggered drip is gustatory rhinitis.

Capsaicin does not dissolve well in water, which is why a sip of water may spread the burning feeling around rather than end it. Dairy foods can help because a milk protein called casein is better at pulling capsaicin away from receptors.

Spicy food creates a curious disagreement: your tongue reports a fire, your brain sends the cleanup crew, and the thermometer calmly insists that nothing is burning.
