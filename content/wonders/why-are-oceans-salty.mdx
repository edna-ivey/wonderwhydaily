Take a sip of ocean water and it tastes as if the sea has been seasoning itself for ages. In a way, it has.

Rain and rivers slowly dissolve minerals from rock and carry their ions toward the sea. Ocean water evaporates into the atmosphere, but the dissolved salts stay behind, so they can build up over enormous stretches of time.

Most ocean salt is made of sodium and chloride, but seawater contains many other dissolved ingredients too. The particular mix comes from rock weathering, underwater vents, volcanoes, and chemical exchanges with the seafloor.

The ocean does not simply get saltier forever. Sea life, seafloor minerals, and chemical reactions remove some ions too. Today's saltiness reflects a long-running balance between ingredients arriving and leaving.

The ocean is a giant mixing bowl with no easy way for dissolved salt to evaporate out.

A glass of seawater holds a small sample of a planet-wide recipe that has been stirred by rivers and currents for immense stretches of time.
