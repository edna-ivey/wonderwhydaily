A black hole is the universe's ultimate hidden heavyweight. You cannot see the black hole itself, yet it can toss stars around, heat nearby gas until it blazes, and bend passing light.

We see objects when light reaches our eyes or telescopes. A black hole has a boundary called the event horizon. Cross that boundary, and gravity has curved space so steeply that every possible path leads farther inward. Light cannot travel back out to tell us what is there, so the black hole remains truly dark.

That does not mean the neighborhood is quiet. Gas and dust falling toward a black hole can whirl into a scorching disk outside the event horizon. The disk may glow brighter than entire groups of stars. That brilliant ring is not the black hole; it is matter heating up before reaching the point of no return.

Astronomers can also spot stars orbiting an apparently empty place, detect hot material racing around it, or measure ripples in space created when black holes collide. It is like discovering an invisible dancer by watching every partner spin.

A black hole hides its own light perfectly, but it cannot hide its influence. The emptiest-looking object in the universe can reveal itself by making the space around it impossible to ignore.
