High above the ground, your phone may desperately try to introduce itself to cell towers racing past far below. Airplane mode tells it to stop shouting.

A normal phone regularly sends and receives radio signals to stay connected with a cellular network. On the ground, it usually communicates with a manageable number of nearby towers. From an airplane, it can see signals from many towers across a huge area and move between them quickly. That creates unnecessary network chatter and can drain the phone's battery as it keeps searching.

Airlines and regulators also want to reduce any chance that many transmitting devices could interfere with sensitive aircraft equipment. Modern planes are designed and tested carefully, so one forgotten phone is not expected to send an airplane tumbling from the sky. Airplane mode is a simple, consistent precaution that quiets cellular radios during flight.

The name can be misleading because the mode does not have to make a device completely disconnected. On many flights, passengers may turn Wi-Fi or Bluetooth back on after enabling airplane mode. The aircraft's onboard Wi-Fi then handles the connection through approved equipment.

Airplane mode is less like switching your phone off and more like asking it to use its indoor voice while traveling through the sky.
