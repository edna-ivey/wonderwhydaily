A popcorn kernel is a snack-sized pressure cooker waiting for exactly the right moment to explode.

A popcorn kernel traps a little water inside a hard outer shell. Heat turns the water into steam, pressure rises, and the shell finally bursts. The softened starch rushes outward and cools into the fluffy shape you eat.

The burst flips the kernel inside out. What began as tightly packed starch becomes a rapidly expanding foam, and that foam freezes into place as it cools in the air.

Not every corn kernel can become popcorn. Popcorn has a hull strong enough to hold pressure and the right kind of dense starch inside. Sweet corn and field corn are built differently.

The white puff is the kernel turned inside out, a tiny burst of steam transforming hard starch into edible foam.

For a moment, the steam pressure inside the kernel is stronger than its shell, and dinner arrives with a bang.
