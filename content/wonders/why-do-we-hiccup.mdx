A hiccup is a two-part surprise: first your breathing muscle jumps, then your throat slams the door on the incoming air.

A hiccup begins when the diaphragm, the main breathing muscle below your lungs, suddenly spasms. A moment later, the opening between your vocal cords snaps shut and stops the incoming air, creating the familiar hic sound.

The spasm and the vocal-cord closure happen in a quick sequence controlled by nerves. Irritation from eating fast, fizzy drinks, laughter, or a sudden temperature change can sometimes set that little reflex loop going. The sound is the reflex's final snap.

Hiccups start before birth. Ultrasound can show fetuses hiccupping in the womb, where the movement may help developing breathing muscles practice even though no air is being breathed.

That tiny hic sound is the audible ending of a reflex that races through muscles and nerves before you can vote against it.

You cannot politely ask the reflex to stop, which is why hiccup cures usually try to interrupt its rhythm instead.
