A QR code looks like somebody spilled a box of black-and-white confetti. Yet your phone can glance at that mess from the side, straighten it out, and read it in less than a second.

The secret starts with the three big squares in the corners. They are like bright flags shouting, "The code is over here, and this is how it is turned." Once the camera finds them, software can map the tilted shape back into a neat square grid. Smaller alignment marks help when the code is curved or seen at a steep angle. Then the phone checks which tiny squares are dark and which are light, turning the pattern into information.

A QR code is not just carrying a message. It is carrying instructions for how to find and read that message. That is why rotating the page does not confuse it the way rotating a line of ordinary writing might.

The name means Quick Response, and the format was invented in Japan to track auto parts moving through factories. It was built for a world where labels, objects, and cameras would not politely hold still. Every scan is a tiny rescue mission: find the square, flatten the view, and uncover the message hiding in the mosaic.
