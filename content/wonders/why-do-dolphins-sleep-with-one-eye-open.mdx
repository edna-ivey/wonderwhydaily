For a dolphin, falling completely unconscious would be risky. It lives in water but breathes air, so it must keep returning to the surface on purpose.

Dolphins solve this problem with unihemispheric sleep: one half of the brain rests while the other remains active. The awake half helps control surfacing and breathing, watches for danger, and keeps the dolphin oriented near its group. The eye connected to that alert half usually stays open.

Later, the two sides switch jobs. The rested half wakes, the busy half gets its turn to sleep, and the other eye may open. It is as if the brain runs a night shift without ever closing the whole building.

Young dolphins and their mothers may remain especially active after birth. Calves need frequent access to the surface and protection while learning to move with the group.

Humans cannot divide sleep this way; our brains move through sleep as a more unified system. A dolphin's half-awake rest sounds impossible because it is built for a different world. Even while sleeping, the animal must remember the surface, the pod, and the next breath.

For a dolphin, staying alive means never letting the entire brain leave the water unattended.
