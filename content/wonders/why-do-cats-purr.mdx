A purring cat sounds like it has a tiny, perfectly tuned motor hidden somewhere behind its ribs.

A repeating nerve signal makes muscles around a cat's voice box tighten and relax. Air passing during both inhaling and exhaling creates the steady vibration we recognize as a purr.

The vibration is produced with remarkable efficiency. Unlike a meow, it can continue across the whole breathing cycle, which is why a relaxed cat can create that tiny engine sound for such a long time. A purr's rhythm is built into the breathing machinery itself.

Purring does not always mean happiness. Cats may also purr while frightened, injured, giving birth, or asking for something, so the rest of the cat's body language matters.

A purr is less like a single word meaning happy and more like a whole tone of voice whose meaning depends on the moment.

The sound can fill a quiet room, yet it begins with movements so small that you cannot see them from outside the cat.
