## Green is expensive

Leaves look green because they contain chlorophyll, the pigment that helps capture light for photosynthesis. Chlorophyll is useful, but it is also costly for a tree to keep making.

As autumn days grow shorter, many trees prepare for winter. They slow chlorophyll production and begin pulling valuable nutrients out of their leaves before letting them go.

## Hidden colors step forward

When the green chlorophyll breaks down, yellow and orange pigments called carotenoids become visible. They were often in the leaf all along, helping with light absorption and protection.

Some trees also make red and purple pigments called anthocyanins during autumn. Scientists are still studying exactly why, but the pigments may help protect leaves while the tree recovers nutrients.

## A carefully managed goodbye

The color change is not simply a leaf dying from cold. It is part of an organized process that lets a tree save what it can and seal off each leaf before winter.
