An autumn tree can look as if someone arrived overnight with buckets of red, orange, and gold paint. But some of those colors were inside the leaves the whole summer, waiting for green to step aside.

During the growing season, leaves make plenty of chlorophyll, the green pigment that captures light for photosynthesis. Chlorophyll is useful but it does not last forever, so trees keep replacing it. As days grow shorter, a tree begins preparing for winter. It stops making as much chlorophyll and pulls useful materials back out of its leaves. As the green fades, yellow and orange pigments that were already there finally become visible.

Red is a different twist. Some trees make red and purple pigments in autumn, often using sugars trapped in the leaf. So a fall leaf can be both a reveal and a last-minute art project.

Each leaf is a tiny solar panel the tree is carefully taking apart before winter. That is why the show varies from year to year and even from branch to branch. Light, temperature, and water all affect the timing. Autumn color is not simply leaves dying. It is a tree tidying up, saving what it can, and letting hidden colors have one brilliant turn in the spotlight.
