Crack a raw egg into a bowl and it slips and spreads like a thick liquid. Add heat, and the same material becomes firm enough to slice. Nothing new has been poured in; the proteins have changed shape.

Raw egg proteins are long chains folded into compact forms and suspended in water. Heat makes those chains wiggle vigorously and unfold. Once opened, they bump into neighboring proteins and form new connections.

As more proteins link together, they create a three-dimensional network that traps water. The egg can no longer flow freely, so it changes from liquid to soft solid. The clear egg white also becomes white because the new protein network scatters light.

More heat creates more connections. Cook an egg gently and the network remains tender. Cook it too long or too hot and the tightening proteins squeeze out water, producing a rubbery white or dry yolk.

The process is called coagulation, and it appears throughout cooking, from custard to scrambled eggs. Heat does not simply “dry out” the egg. It rearranges invisible folded chains until they catch one another, turning a loose crowd of proteins into an edible net.
