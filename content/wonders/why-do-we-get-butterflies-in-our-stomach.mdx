Your stomach does not contain nervous insects, but before a performance, first day, or exciting surprise, it can certainly feel as if something is fluttering inside.

That sensation begins with the connection between your brain and digestive system. When you feel nervous or thrilled, the autonomic nervous system prepares the body to respond. Stress hormones rise, the heart may beat faster, and blood flow can shift away from digestion toward muscles that might need quick energy.

The muscles of the stomach and intestines also change their movement. Because the gut contains a huge network of nerves, you can feel those changes as fluttering, tightening, dropping, or even mild nausea.

Excitement and fear can create similar physical signals. A roller coaster and a difficult conversation may both make your heart race, even though your thoughts about them are completely different. Your brain interprets the situation; your body activates much of the same alert system.

Slow breathing can sometimes calm the feeling by signaling that immediate action is not necessary. Butterflies are not proof that something will go badly. They are evidence that your body has noticed this moment matters and is getting ready to meet it.
