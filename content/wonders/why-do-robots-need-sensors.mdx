Imagine trying to cross a room with no sight, hearing, touch, or sense of balance. Even perfect instructions such as "walk forward" would become risky because you could not notice a chair, a wall, or your own wrong turn.

A robot faces the same problem without sensors. Its motors can move wheels, arms, or joints, but movement alone does not reveal what happened. Sensors translate pieces of the physical world into data the robot's computer can use. Cameras measure light. Distance sensors detect nearby objects. Touch sensors notice contact. Encoders track how far a wheel or joint has turned.

That information creates a feedback loop. A robot moves, checks the result, and adjusts. A warehouse robot can slow when someone steps into its path. A robotic arm can stop squeezing when it detects enough force. A small rolling robot can correct when one wheel travels farther than the other.

Sensors do not make a robot magically aware. Engineers still decide what measurements matter and write rules or learning systems for responding to them. A sensor can also be fooled by glare, dirt, darkness, or an object it was not designed to detect.

Robots need sensors because useful action depends on noticing the difference between what was supposed to happen and what actually happened.
