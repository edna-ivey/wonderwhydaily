Biting a lemon can make your whole face fold inward before you decide to react. That instant pucker begins with tiny charged particles meeting specialized taste cells.

Lemons contain plenty of citric acid. In the watery environment of your mouth, that acid releases hydrogen ions. Sour-sensing cells in your taste buds detect those ions and send signals through nerves to your brain. Your brain interprets the message as sourness, and your mouth may produce extra saliva to dilute and clear the acid.

The reaction is useful. Strong sourness can warn us about unripe fruit or food that has changed through fermentation or spoilage. But sour does not automatically mean unsafe. Humans also enjoy controlled sourness in lemonade, yogurt, pickles, candy, and many other foods.

Taste is more than a simple measurement of acid. Smell, temperature, sweetness, and expectation all change the experience. Add sugar to lemon juice and the acid remains, but the sweetness balances its sharp edge enough to create a completely different drink.

A lemon does not contain “sour flavor” as a separate ingredient. It carries molecules that start a conversation with your taste buds, and your brain turns that electrical message into one of the boldest sensations on the menu.
