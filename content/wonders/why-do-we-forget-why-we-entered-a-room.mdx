You march into a room with a mission, cross the doorway, and discover that the mission has vanished.

Working memory holds a small, fragile set of current goals. Crossing a doorway changes the surrounding context, and the brain may treat that boundary as the start of a new event, making the previous goal harder to retrieve.

The doorway is not magically erasing your mind. It is one of many scene changes that can make the brain update what matters now, allowing a small unfinished intention to slip out of the spotlight.

Walking back can help because the old room restores useful memory cues. You are not reversing the forgetting; you are giving your brain the setting connected to the original thought.

The doorway did not erase your thought. It simply encouraged your brain to turn the page before one small unfinished sentence was saved.

The lost goal often returns when you go back because the old room restores the sights and context that surrounded the original thought.
