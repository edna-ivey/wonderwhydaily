Chocolate performs a delicious trick: it stays firm on the table, then seems to surrender the moment it reaches your tongue.

Cocoa butter stays solid around normal room temperature but melts near body temperature. That narrow melting range lets chocolate keep a firm shape in your hand, then soften quickly on your tongue.

The recipe changes the experience too. Milk fat, sugar, and cocoa solids affect how quickly a piece softens and how it feels, while the cocoa butter provides the dramatic solid-to-silky transition. A few degrees can completely change the texture. That narrow window is part of chocolate's charm.

Cocoa butter can form several crystal structures. Chocolate makers use tempering to encourage the form that looks glossy, snaps cleanly, and melts smoothly.

That smooth melt is not an accident. It comes from a fat whose favorite melting temperature happens to be very close to yours.

That is why a piece can snap crisply between your fingers and become a glossy pool only moments later.
