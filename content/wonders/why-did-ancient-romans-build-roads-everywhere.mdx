The Roman Empire stretched across enormous distances, and an order from the capital was useless if it took forever to arrive. Roads became the empire's circulation system.

Roman engineers built routes that allowed soldiers to move quickly toward trouble, officials to travel between provinces, and messengers to carry government news. Merchants and ordinary travelers used the same networks, helping goods, ideas, and languages move alongside armies.

The roads were not all identical stone highways. Builders adapted them to local terrain and available materials. Important routes could include carefully prepared foundations, drainage ditches, milestones, bridges, and durable surfaces. A road that shed water and resisted mud remained useful in weather that could stop an unprepared path.

Roads also made Roman power visible. Milestones named emperors, and straight stretches announced that engineers had imposed an organized route across the landscape.

The famous saying that all roads lead to Rome was never literally true, but it captures the network's purpose. Roads connected distant places to the empire's centers of authority. Long after Roman rule ended, many routes remained useful because a well-chosen path can outlive the government that built it.

Some journeys today still follow decisions made by Roman engineers.
