A beach does not arrive with a standard sand color. It wears the story of the rocks nearby.

Many black beaches sit near volcanic landscapes. When lava cools quickly, it can form dark rocks such as basalt. Waves pound cliffs and chunks of lava, while wind, rain, and tumbling stones help break the material into smaller and smaller pieces. Eventually, dark mineral grains collect along the shore.

Fresh lava entering the ocean can also shatter as intensely hot rock meets cold water. The pieces are then sorted and reshaped by waves. Some grains remain rough and glassy; others become smoother after years of movement.

Black sand can become extremely hot in sunlight because its dark surface absorbs lots of energy. It may also be temporary. A powerful storm can carry an entire layer offshore, and later currents may return it or build the beach somewhere else.

White tropical sand often comes largely from shells and coral. Tan sand may contain quartz and other light minerals. Black sand is a dramatic reminder that every beach is borrowed geology. The shoreline is simply where waves pause long enough to display the pieces.
