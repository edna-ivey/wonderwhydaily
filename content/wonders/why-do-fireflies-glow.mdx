A firefly is carrying a tiny lantern, but there is no bulb, battery, or flame inside. Its light comes from a carefully controlled chemical reaction.

In a special organ near the abdomen, a molecule called luciferin reacts with oxygen. An enzyme called luciferase helps the reaction happen, and the released energy appears as visible light. The firefly can control oxygen reaching the light-producing cells, allowing it to switch its glow on and off in precise flashes.

Those flashes are messages. Many adult fireflies use distinct patterns to find mates of their own species. One may flash while flying, and another may answer from the grass. In a dark field filled with several species, different rhythms help keep the conversations from becoming one enormous blinking mix-up.

Firefly larvae can glow too, and their light may warn predators that they taste unpleasant. So the same remarkable ability can serve as both an invitation and a warning sign.

Most of the reaction's energy becomes light instead of heat. A firefly is not carrying a hot little furnace; it is producing cool light with an efficiency human-made lamps struggle to match. On a summer night, every blink is chemistry speaking in code.
