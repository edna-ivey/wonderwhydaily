Mercury sits closest to the Sun, so it seems obvious that it should win the solar system's hottest-planet contest. Venus wins instead, and it is not even close.

The difference is atmosphere. Mercury has almost none. Sunlight heats its surface intensely during the day, but with no thick blanket of gases, much of that heat can escape into space after darkness arrives. Mercury swings between scorching days and brutally cold nights.

Venus is wrapped in an atmosphere about ninety times denser than Earth's, made mostly of carbon dioxide. Sunlight reaches the surface and warms it. The planet then tries to send that energy back upward as heat, but the dense atmosphere absorbs and redirects much of it. Heat becomes trapped in a powerful greenhouse effect.

Imagine Mercury as a sun-warmed rock left outside after sunset. Venus is more like that rock sealed inside an enormous insulating oven that never gets switched off.

Clouds cover Venus, but they do not make the surface pleasantly shady. Beneath them, the thick atmosphere spreads heat around the planet so effectively that nights remain fiercely hot too.

Venus reveals a surprising planetary rule: where a world lives matters, but what surrounds it can completely rewrite the result. A planet farther from the Sun can become hotter simply by refusing to let the heat leave.
