The air looks clear. Sunlight looks white. So where does an entire sky full of blue come from?

White sunlight is really a bundle of many colors traveling together. When it enters Earth's atmosphere, it bumps into tiny air molecules. Those molecules scatter shorter wavelengths of light much more strongly than longer ones. Blue light gets tossed around the sky like a pinball, arriving at your eyes from every direction. That scattered light is the blue you see overhead.

The sky is not a blue ceiling, and it is not borrowing its color from the ocean. You are looking at sunlight being redirected by countless invisible molecules. Every patch of blue is light that changed course before reaching you.

Near sunset, sunlight must travel through much more atmosphere to reach your eyes. Along that longer journey, much of the blue light scatters away from the direct beam. Reds and oranges are left to stream through, which is why the same clear air can create a blue afternoon and a fiery evening.

The atmosphere is basically sorting sunlight by color all day long. Blue gets scattered across the wide sky; sunset reveals more of what travels straight through. The color above you is a record of the route sunlight took to reach your eyes.
