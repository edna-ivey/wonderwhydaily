Inside a computer, every photo, song, game, and message begins with a choice so simple it sounds almost silly: on or off.

Electronic circuits can reliably tell two voltage ranges apart, treating them as off and on, or zero and one. Long patterns of those bits can represent numbers, letters, pictures, sounds, and instructions.

Software builds enormous variety from those two states by grouping bits into patterns. The hardware does not see a photo or a song; it switches billions of tiny components according to the encoded instructions. Simple switches become useful through speed and scale.

Binary is not the only possible number system for computing. It became dominant because two clearly separated electrical states are easier to build and protect from noise than many delicate states.

Two tiny states are enough to build digital worlds because the real magic comes from arranging them at astonishing speed.

The simplicity makes the signals sturdy: a circuit can tolerate a little electrical mess and still decide whether it received a zero or a one.
