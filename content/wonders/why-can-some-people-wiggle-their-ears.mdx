A cat can swivel its ears toward a faint noise while keeping its head still. Human ears mostly stay put, but a few people can make them perform a tiny echo of that movement.

Small auricular muscles surround the outer ear. In many mammals, these muscles aim the ears toward sounds and help the animal gather information. Humans still have the muscles, but they are usually weak and rarely under conscious control.

Some people can activate them naturally. Others may learn a small amount of movement by discovering how ear motion connects with nearby scalp or facial muscles. Genetics, muscle development, and practice may all influence the ability.

The skill does not mean an ear wiggler hears dramatically better. Human ears cannot rotate enough to function like a cat's, and we usually turn our heads instead.

Ear wiggling is often called vestigial behavior: a reduced trace of something more useful in ancestors. “Vestigial” does not always mean completely useless or disappearing. It can simply mean a feature no longer performs its old job at the same scale. A wiggling ear is a quiet little message from a more mobile past.
