Laughter can burst out before you have decided whether something is funny, and sometimes nothing resembling a joke was involved.

Laughter is a coordinated social signal involving the brain, breathing, voice, and face. It often appears during play, relief, surprise, or friendly conversation, not only after a carefully constructed joke.

The sound can spread through a group before everyone knows what started it. Hearing another person laugh primes your own laughter system, helping a room of separate people begin reacting together. Laughter can communicate belonging without a sentence. It is conversation made from breath and sound.

People laugh much more often with others than alone. That makes laughter a kind of social glue that can show connection, soften awkwardness, or invite everyone into the same mood.

Laughter is not only a reaction to humor. It is one of the sounds humans use to tune a whole group to the same moment.
