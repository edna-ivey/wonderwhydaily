Zebra stripes are so bold that they seem like camouflage designed by someone who forgot to hide the animal. Their strongest benefit may involve a much smaller enemy: biting flies.

Scientists have tested many ideas, including cooling, social recognition, and confusing predators. Evidence most strongly supports protection from flies such as horseflies and tsetse flies. These insects can spread disease and cause painful bites.

When flies approach striped surfaces, they appear to have trouble controlling their landing. They may come in too fast, veer away, or fail to settle properly. The sharp contrast between black and white could disrupt the visual information they use during the final approach.

That does not mean stripes serve only one purpose. They may also help zebras recognize one another, and researchers continue studying how the pattern affects heat and group movement. Evolution can preserve a feature that offers several advantages.

The stripes are not simply painted onto identical animals. Each zebra carries its own pattern. To human eyes it is a spectacular coat; to a hungry biting fly, it may be a landing strip that refuses to stay visually still.

A bold pattern can be defensive without hiding anything at all.
