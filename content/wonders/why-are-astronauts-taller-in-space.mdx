An astronaut can leave Earth at one height and discover that their spacesuit fits a little differently in orbit. They have not entered a sudden growth spurt. Their spine has stretched.

Your backbone is a stack of bones called vertebrae, separated by soft discs that cushion movement. On Earth, gravity and everyday upright activity gently compress those discs. In microgravity, the spine no longer carries the body's weight in the same way, so the discs absorb fluid and expand.

The change can add several centimeters to an astronaut's height. It may sound delightful, but the stretching can cause back discomfort, and spacecraft equipment must be designed to accommodate it.

Microgravity changes much more than height. Without regular weight-bearing, muscles weaken and bones can lose density. Astronauts exercise for hours to help protect their bodies during long missions.

After returning to Earth, gravity begins compressing the spine again, and the borrowed height disappears. The astronaut has not grown new bone or permanently become taller. Space simply reveals that your everyday height is partly a negotiation between your flexible body and the constant pull beneath your feet.

Even your height is less fixed than it seems.
