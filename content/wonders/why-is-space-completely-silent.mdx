Imagine watching two asteroids smash together with enough force to carve a crater. You would see debris burst outward, but if you were somehow floating nearby, you would hear absolutely nothing. Space can stage a spectacular event and forget to include the soundtrack.

Sound is a traveling vibration. When you clap, your hands shove nearby air molecules. Those molecules bump into their neighbors, passing the vibration along until it reaches your ears. It is a little like a stadium wave: the pattern moves because people are close enough to pass it onward.

Most of space is a near-vacuum. It is not perfectly empty, but its particles are spread so far apart that they cannot efficiently pass a sound wave from one to the next. A star may erupt, a planet may collide, and a rocket engine may blast hot gas, yet no ordinary sound can cross the emptiness to reach a listener.

That does not make space peaceful. Radio waves, light, and other forms of energy travel through a vacuum just fine, so scientists can translate signals from space into sounds we can hear. Those eerie recordings are useful interpretations, not microphones capturing cosmic noise.

Space is full of violent motion. Its silence comes from the astonishing emptiness between everything.
