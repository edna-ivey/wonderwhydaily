Ask someone to draw the Sun and they will probably reach for yellow. Seen from space, however, the Sun appears white.

Sunlight contains the full range of visible colors. Mixed together, those colors look white. Before sunlight reaches your eyes on the ground, it travels through Earth's atmosphere. Tiny molecules scatter shorter blue wavelengths more strongly than longer red and yellow wavelengths.

When the Sun is high, it can still look nearly white, though its brightness and the surrounding blue sky influence how we perceive it. Near sunrise or sunset, sunlight travels through much more atmosphere. More blue and green light is scattered away from the direct path, leaving the Sun looking orange or red.

The Sun itself has not changed color during the day. The atmosphere has changed which parts of its light reach you directly.

This is also connected to the blue sky: some of the blue light removed from the Sun's direct beam is scattered across the sky around us. One white star provides both a yellow-looking daytime Sun and a blue dome overhead, simply because its light takes different routes through the air.

The atmosphere is quietly remixing sunlight all day long.
