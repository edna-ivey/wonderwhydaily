Imagine leaving a footprint and returning thousands of generations later to find its outline still waiting. On Earth, wind, rain, flowing water, plants, and animals would usually erase it much sooner.

The Moon has almost none of those footprint erasers. Its atmosphere is extremely thin, so there is no ordinary wind or weather. No rain falls, and no streams run across the ground. When astronauts stepped into the powdery lunar soil, their boots pressed grains into crisp shapes, and very little began smoothing those shapes afterward.

That does not make the prints permanent. The Moon is continually struck by space rocks. Countless tiny impacts slowly stir and mix the top layer of soil, a process sometimes called impact gardening. A direct hit could erase a footprint quickly, while smaller impacts may gradually soften its outline over a very long time.

Scientists cannot attach an exact expiration date to each bootprint. Estimates measured in millions of years describe how slowly the surface changes without weather, not a guarantee that every detail will survive untouched.

The famous prints are less like marks sealed inside a museum and more like writing left in a place where erasers are rare. They may outlast civilizations, yet the quiet Moon is still changing one tiny impact at a time.
