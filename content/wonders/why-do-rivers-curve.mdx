A river has somewhere to go, yet it almost never takes the straightest route. It wanders as if it has time to explore.

A tiny bend sends faster water toward the outside bank, where it erodes soil. Slower water on the inside drops sediment. That difference strengthens the bend, so the river gradually develops larger curves.

Once a bend begins, the flow itself makes it stronger. Water spirals through the channel, carrying loosened material away from the outer bank and helping deposit it on the quieter inner bank. The river redraws its own route as it travels.

River bends do not stay put. They slowly migrate across floodplains, and a looping bend can eventually be cut off from the main channel to form an oxbow lake.

A bend is not just a shape in a river. It is the river actively editing its own path.

Over enough time, a river can move so far sideways that old maps begin to look like drawings of a different place.
