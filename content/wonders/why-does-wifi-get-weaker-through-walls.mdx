Wi-Fi can cross a room invisibly, but give it a thick wall and suddenly your video begins thinking very slowly.

Wi-Fi travels as radio waves. Walls can absorb, reflect, and scatter some of that energy before it reaches your device. Dense materials, metal, water, and greater distance usually weaken the signal most.

A signal can reach you by several paths after bouncing around a room. Those copies may strengthen or cancel one another, which is why moving a device just a short distance can suddenly improve reception. A room has invisible radio-wave bright and dim spots.

The frequency band matters too. Higher-frequency Wi-Fi can carry lots of data but often struggles more with walls, while lower-frequency signals usually travel farther and pass through obstacles better.

Your home is full of invisible radio echoes, shadows, and dead spots that can change when you move only a few steps.

The signal does not simply stop at a wall; pieces of it may pass through, bounce away, or arrive weakened and scrambled.
