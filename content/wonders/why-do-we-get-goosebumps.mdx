A song reaches its biggest moment, a cold breeze slips under your sleeve, and suddenly your skin looks like a tiny field of bumps. Each bump is a small muscle making an ancient move.

At the base of every body hair is a microscopic muscle called the arrector pili. When your nervous system sends the signal, those muscles contract and tug the hairs upright. The pulled skin rises around each follicle, creating goosebumps.

For furry animals, this reaction is practical. Raised fur traps a thicker layer of insulating air during cold weather. It can also make a frightened animal look larger to a threat. Humans kept the reflex even though our relatively sparse body hair is not thick enough to create a useful winter coat or an especially intimidating silhouette.

Strong emotions can trigger the same automatic system. Fear, awe, surprise, and music can activate brain circuits connected to alertness and arousal. Your body reacts before you have time to vote on the matter.

Goosebumps are a visible message from a much older version of the human body. They may no longer fluff us into impressive furry creatures, but they still reveal moments when something has reached us deeply enough to make our skin stand up and notice.
