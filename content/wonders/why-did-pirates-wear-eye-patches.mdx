The pirate eye patch has become so famous that it is easy to forget a wonderfully awkward fact: history never promised us one tidy reason for it.

Some pirates certainly covered injured or missing eyes. The popular idea that patches preserved one eye's night vision below deck is scientifically plausible, but historians do not have strong evidence that pirates commonly used them that way.

Human eyes need time to become highly sensitive in darkness. Covering one eye could preserve that sensitivity, but the famous pirate explanation remains a clever possibility rather than a well-documented everyday practice.

This is a good example of a story that can work without being proven. A successful experiment shows that the night-vision trick is possible; it cannot show that historical pirates actually practiced it.

Sometimes the most satisfying historical answer is not a dramatic yes or no, but learning exactly where evidence ends and a clever story begins.
