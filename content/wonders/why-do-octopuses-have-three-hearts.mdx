An octopus looks like one flowing creature, but inside it carries a three-pump circulation team.

Two of its hearts sit near the gills. Their job is to push blood through those gills so it can collect oxygen from the water. The third heart, called the systemic heart, takes that oxygen-rich blood and sends it around the rest of the body. Instead of asking one heart to manage every part of the trip, the octopus divides the work.

That division matters because pushing blood through an octopus's gills takes effort. The two gill hearts help move blood through that demanding section, while the main heart keeps the arms, organs, and powerful brain supplied.

Here is the strange part: when an octopus swims by blasting water from its body like a jet, its main heart slows dramatically and may briefly stop beating. Swimming is tiring, so octopuses often prefer to crawl along the seafloor when they can.

An octopus is already famous for eight arms, shape-shifting skin, and remarkable problem-solving. Hidden beneath all that is another surprise: a coordinated trio of hearts quietly keeping the whole extraordinary animal moving.

Three hearts are not an extravagant bonus. They are a clever answer to the challenge of delivering oxygen through a body built unlike almost any other.
