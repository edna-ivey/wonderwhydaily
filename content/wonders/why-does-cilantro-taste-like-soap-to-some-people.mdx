One person sprinkles cilantro onto a taco and tastes something bright and fresh. Another takes the same bite and wonders who added hand soap. Neither person is pretending.

Much of flavor comes from smell. Cilantro releases several aroma molecules called aldehydes. Some smell green or citrusy; others resemble compounds found in soaps and lotions. Genetic differences in odor receptors can make certain people especially sensitive to those soap-like notes.

Genes are not the entire story. Culture, repeated exposure, freshness, and the other ingredients in a dish can all influence whether cilantro feels pleasant. Some people who initially dislike it become more comfortable with the flavor over time, especially when it is chopped or blended with other foods.

This is why taste arguments are often impossible to settle. Two people can eat the same leaf while their sensory systems highlight different parts of its chemical mixture.

Cilantro is not secretly turning into soap on one person's plate. It is more like a song with many instruments: one listener hears the bright melody, while another cannot stop noticing the strange note playing underneath.

One leaf can genuinely create two very different sensory experiences.
