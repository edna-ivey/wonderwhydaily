A busy Wi-Fi network is like a lively dinner table where only one person can speak clearly at a time. The more devices joining the conversation, the more carefully everyone must take turns.

Wi-Fi uses radio waves to move data between devices and a router. Devices on the same channel share a limited amount of airtime. A phone downloading a game, a television streaming video, and a laptop joining a call all need chances to send and receive information.

The router coordinates this traffic, but coordination itself takes time. If signals collide or arrive poorly, data may need to be sent again. Older or distant devices can also occupy the channel longer because they communicate more slowly.

Then there is the internet connection beyond the router. Even if the local Wi-Fi works perfectly, every device may still share one limited connection from the internet provider.

Modern routers use multiple bands, channels, and clever scheduling to keep traffic moving. Still, no system has endless capacity. When a crowded network feels sluggish, the signal has not become tired. Many invisible conversations are simply waiting for their turn to speak.

The slowdown is a traffic jam made entirely of messages.
