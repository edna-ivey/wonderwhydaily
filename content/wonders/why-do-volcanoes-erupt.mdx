A volcano is not simply a mountain that catches fire. It is a place where Earth's hot interior finds a doorway to the surface.

Below the crust, melted or partly melted rock called magma can rise because it is less dense than the solid rock around it. Magma may collect underground, carrying dissolved gases along with it. As it rises and the pressure above it decreases, those gases expand, a little like bubbles appearing when a fizzy drink is opened.

If the magma and gas can push through cracks or weak places in the crust, an eruption begins. Sometimes runny magma lets gas escape easily and lava pours out in glowing streams. Sometimes thick, sticky magma traps gas until pressure builds and the eruption breaks rock and magma apart into ash and fragments.

The volcano's shape, the magma's chemistry, and the amount of trapped gas all help decide whether an eruption will ooze, burst, or do something in between. Two volcanoes can be powered by the same basic ingredients and still behave completely differently.

An eruption is Earth releasing material and pressure from below, but it is also construction. Layer after layer of cooled lava and ash can build islands, mountains, and entirely new ground. A volcano can destroy a landscape and create the next one at the same time.
