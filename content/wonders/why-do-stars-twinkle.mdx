A star can shine steadily for billions of years and still look as if it is nervously flickering just for you.

Starlight passes through moving layers of air with different temperatures and densities. Those layers bend the light by slightly changing amounts from moment to moment, making the star seem to jump, brighten, and dim.

The star itself is usually shining steadily during all this flickering. Your view is wobbling because the atmosphere acts like a constantly changing stack of weak lenses between the star and your eye.

Planets usually twinkle less because they appear as tiny disks rather than single points. Light from different parts of the disk gets bent differently, and those wobbles tend to average one another out.

The twinkle is not the star changing its mind. It is Earth's restless atmosphere scribbling on the light during the final part of its journey.

By the time that wobbling light reaches you, it may have crossed space for years and spent its final instant being jostled by air.
