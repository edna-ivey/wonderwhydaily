Your phone does not look down from space and recognize your jacket. It solves a very fast timing puzzle.

GPS satellites continuously broadcast where they are and precisely when each signal left. Those radio signals race toward Earth at the speed of light. Your phone compares their arrival times to estimate how far away each satellite is. One distance gives many possible locations. Several distances crossing together narrow the answer to one small area.

It is like hearing bells ring from several towers at known times and using the delays to work out where you must be standing. The difference is that the delays are unbelievably tiny, and the towers are moving thousands of kilometers above you.

Phones often make the answer faster and steadier by checking nearby Wi-Fi networks, cell towers, motion sensors, and a compass. Indoors or among tall buildings, satellite signals can bounce or weaken, so those extra clues matter.

Location access is controlled through app permissions because this useful trick can also reveal sensitive information about where you go. The blue dot on a map looks simple, but underneath it is a conversation among orbiting clocks, radio waves, nearby networks, and a pocket computer solving geometry in seconds.
