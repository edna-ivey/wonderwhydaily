## A fast temperature change

Cold food or drink can rapidly chill the roof of your mouth and the back of your throat. Nearby blood vessels respond quickly, and sensory nerves report the sudden change.

One important messenger is the trigeminal nerve, which carries sensation from much of your face as well as parts of your mouth.

## Your brain guesses the wrong address

The brain is not always perfect at locating pain from internal structures. It interprets this burst of nerve activity as if it came from another area served by the same nerve, often the forehead.

This is called referred pain: the signal begins in one place but feels as though it comes from somewhere else.

## Unpleasant, brief, and harmless

Brain freeze usually peaks quickly and disappears within a minute or two. Eating cold foods more slowly gives your mouth time to adjust and makes the dramatic false alarm less likely.
