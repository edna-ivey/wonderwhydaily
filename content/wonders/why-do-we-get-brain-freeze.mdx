One second you are enjoying ice cream. The next, an invisible thumb seems to press sharply into your forehead. The odd part is that the ice cream never went anywhere near your forehead.

Brain freeze begins when something very cold rapidly chills the roof of your mouth. Nerves and nearby blood vessels react to that sudden temperature change and send an urgent signal along pathways also used by parts of your face. Your brain has to decide where the warning came from, and it sometimes guesses the wrong address. The chill starts in your mouth, but the pain gets pinned to your forehead.

This kind of location mix-up is called referred pain. Your nervous system is less like a perfect street map and more like a busy switchboard where several callers share nearby lines. Most of the time, the operator connects everything correctly. A frozen drink gulped too quickly can make the switchboard briefly panic.

The pain fades because your mouth warms and the alarm quiets down. Eating cold food more slowly gives those sensitive tissues time to adjust. So brain freeze is not your brain becoming frozen at all. It is your body reacting quickly, your nerves sending a warning, and your brain doing its best detective work with a slightly confusing clue.
