Summer once seemed enormous. Later, entire years can feel as if someone pressed fast-forward. Clocks have not changed speed, but the mind does not experience time like a stopwatch.

One reason may be memory. New experiences demand attention and create many distinct details. Looking back, a period packed with firsts can feel long because memory has placed many markers inside it. Familiar routines require less attention and may leave fewer memorable landmarks, so weeks can collapse together.

Scale may matter too. For a ten-year-old, one year equals a tenth of all the life they remember. For a fifty-year-old, it is a much smaller fraction. That comparison may help later years feel less enormous.

Time can even reverse its trick depending on when you judge it. A boring wait may crawl in the moment but nearly disappear in memory. A joyful trip may fly by while happening yet feel rich and long afterward.

There is no single switch making adulthood race. Attention, emotion, routine, and memory all shape the sensation. The clock stays steady; the story your brain builds around its minutes keeps changing.

Novelty cannot slow a clock, but it can give memory more places to pause.
