Before stacks of cheap paper, making a book could begin with an animal hide, a skilled craftsperson, and days of careful work. The result was parchment, a strong writing surface that could carry words for centuries.

To make parchment, workers cleaned and soaked a hide, stretched it tightly on a frame, scraped away hair and tissue, and dried it under tension. The finished sheet was not soft leather. It was thin, firm, and smooth enough for ink, paint, and detailed lettering.

Parchment had important advantages. It could be cut into pages, folded, stitched into books, and written on both sides. In places where papyrus was difficult to obtain or where paper was not yet common, parchment became especially valuable for religious texts, laws, records, and other documents meant to last.

That durability came at a cost. Producing one large book could require the skins of many animals and a huge amount of labor. Pages were valuable enough to reuse. Writers sometimes scraped away an older text and wrote something new over it, creating what is called a palimpsest.

Writing on skin can sound strange today, but it solved a difficult problem: how to give fragile words a physical home strong enough to travel through generations.
