After a long swim, your fingertips look as if they have aged fifty years while the rest of you stayed the same.

After several minutes in water, nerves tell blood vessels beneath your fingertips to narrow. The reduced volume pulls the skin into ridges. It is an active body response, not just skin swelling like a sponge.

The response depends on working nerves, which was a major clue that soaking alone was not the cause. If certain nerves are damaged, the connected fingers may wrinkle much less or not at all. Your fingertips are actively changing their surface.

Wrinkled fingertips may help move water away when gripping wet objects, a little like tire tread. Tests suggest they can improve handling in some wet conditions.

Those temporary ridges are not soggy damage. They are your nervous system deliberately changing the shape of your grip.

Your body quietly redraws the surface of your fingers, then smooths them again after you return to dry land.
