Bread dough begins as a heavy lump and somehow becomes a soft building filled with thousands of tiny rooms.

Yeast eats available sugars and releases carbon dioxide gas. A stretchy gluten network traps that gas in bubbles, so the dough expands instead of letting it escape. Heat then firms the structure into a loaf.

Kneading helps line up and connect gluten proteins into an elastic net. Without a strong enough net, the carbon dioxide slips away; with one, thousands of bubbles stay trapped and lift the dough. Bread is a carefully baked bubble structure.

Bread keeps expanding briefly after it enters the oven. Heat makes trapped gases grow and speeds the yeast for a short time before the yeast stops working and the dough sets.

Every hole in a slice is a bubble that survived long enough for heat to turn a stretchy dough into a lasting structure.

Before baking, those bubbles are soft and movable. In the oven, heat locks their walls into the springy crumb you can slice.
