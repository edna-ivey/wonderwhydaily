Drop an apple into water and it bobs like a tiny red life raft. It looks solid, but a surprising part of an apple is actually air.

Apple flesh is built from cells with small spaces between them. Those spaces help give an apple its crisp bite. Together, they also lower the fruit's average density. Density compares how much material is packed into a certain amount of space. Water can support objects that are less dense than itself, so the apple settles at the surface instead of sinking.

The apple's skin helps keep water from quickly flooding those internal spaces, but the skin is not magically holding the fruit up. The real trick is the combination of plant tissue and trapped air throughout the apple.

This is why weight alone cannot predict whether something floats. A large wooden log may float while a tiny metal coin sinks. What matters is how the object's total mass compares with the space it takes up.

An apple seems compact when you hold it, yet its crisp structure is more like a building filled with countless miniature rooms. Put all those invisible rooms together, and an ordinary snack becomes buoyant enough to play the starring role in apple bobbing.
