A full Moon rising behind trees can look enormous, like it might roll down the street. Hours later, high in the sky, the very same Moon seems to have shrunk.

It has not changed much at all. The effect is called the Moon illusion, and scientists are still studying exactly how the brain produces it. A leading idea is that the horizon provides context. Buildings, mountains, and trees create a scene that feels deep and distant. When the Moon appears beyond that landscape, your brain treats it as extremely far away yet still taking up the same visual angle, so it interprets the Moon as impressively large.

High overhead, the Moon floats in an empty-looking sky without familiar objects nearby. Your brain has fewer clues for judging scale, and the Moon seems smaller.

Earth's atmosphere can make a low Moon look redder or slightly squashed, but it does not magnify it into the giant disk you perceive. You can prove that with a camera or even by comparing the Moon against the width of your fingertip at arm's length.

The Moon illusion is a wonderful reminder that seeing is not simply measuring. Your brain is constantly building a story about size and distance, and sometimes that story makes the sky feel gloriously larger than it is.
