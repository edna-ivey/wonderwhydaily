A knight in full plate armor looks as if one fall would leave them helpless on the ground. In reality, good armor was built for movement as well as protection.

Armor changed across centuries as weapons and fighting styles changed. Mail made from linked metal rings resisted slashing cuts. Later plate armor used shaped steel pieces to deflect blows and protect vulnerable areas from swords, arrows, and lances.

A complete suit was heavy, often comparable to a modern soldier's load, but the weight was distributed across the body rather than hanging from one spot. Hinged joints and overlapping plates allowed trained wearers to walk, run, climb, and mount horses.

Armor did create challenges. It was expensive, hot, tiring over long periods, and could limit vision or breathing depending on the helmet. No suit made a fighter invincible. Opponents developed weapons and techniques aimed at gaps or designed to deliver crushing force.

Knights wore heavy armor for the same reason people accept many uncomfortable safety tools: the alternative was worse. The metal was not ceremonial luggage. It was carefully engineered survival equipment shaped around a moving human body.

Its real achievement was protection that could move with the person inside.
