The ground feels like the definition of stillness, right up until it reminds us that Earth's crust is always under pressure.

Tectonic plates keep moving, but rough fault surfaces can lock together. Rock bends and stores energy until the fault suddenly slips. That release sends seismic waves through the ground, creating the shaking we feel.

The first slip can shake faults far away and change the pressure on nearby rock. That is why one earthquake is often followed by a cluster of smaller ones as the crust settles into a new balance. The ground keeps adjusting after the main jolt.

An earthquake rupture can race along a fault instead of happening at one tiny point. Aftershocks follow as nearby rock adjusts to the new arrangement of stress.

An earthquake is stored movement finally escaping, turning years of invisible strain into seconds you can feel.

The crack does not need to begin beneath your feet. Seismic waves can carry the news of that sudden slip across great distances.
