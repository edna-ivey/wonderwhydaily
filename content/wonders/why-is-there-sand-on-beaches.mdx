A beach feels permanent until you remember that every grain is a traveler with a very long story.

Weather and moving water break rock and shells into smaller pieces. Rivers, waves, and currents carry those grains, then sort and collect them along shorelines according to their size and weight.

Waves are constantly moving the shoreline's grains. Calm water may leave fine sand behind, while strong waves can carry it away and expose heavier pebbles, changing the same beach from season to season. A beach is always being rebuilt under your feet.

A beach records its local ingredients. Volcanic rock can make black sand, coral and shells can make pale sand, and unusual minerals can even create green or pink beaches.

The sand beneath a towel is a temporary gathering of broken mountains, shells, coral, and minerals sorted by moving water.

Waves keep rearranging the collection, carrying some grains away and delivering others, so even a familiar beach is never exactly finished.
