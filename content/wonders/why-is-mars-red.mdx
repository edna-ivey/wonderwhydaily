Mars looks rusty because, on a planet-sized scale, that is almost exactly what happened.

Iron-bearing minerals in Martian rock reacted with oxygen and formed iron oxides, related to rust on Earth. Fine oxidized dust coats the ground and can hang in the atmosphere, spreading the reddish color.

Dust storms can lift that rusty powder high into the thin atmosphere and sometimes spread around much of the planet. The airborne dust changes the color of the Martian sky as well as the view from space. One planet-sized dusting shapes Mars's entire reputation.

Mars is not the same shade everywhere. Close-up images reveal dark volcanic rock, pale dust, and many rusty tones that get blended into one red appearance from far away.

A thin coating of ancient dust is powerful enough to give an entire world its famous color.

If you could brush away the dust in some places, darker volcanic rock would appear beneath the famous rusty surface.
