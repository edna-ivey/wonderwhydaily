Slice an apple and you have started a tiny chemistry countdown. The pale surface is suddenly exposed to a world full of oxygen.

Cutting an apple breaks open cells and lets oxygen meet an enzyme called polyphenol oxidase. The enzyme helps create compounds that combine into brown pigments on the exposed surface.

Acid and low oxygen can slow the enzyme's work. That is why a little lemon juice helps keep cut fruit looking fresh: it changes the conditions rather than painting over the brown color. Refrigeration slows the same chemical teamwork. Covering the cut surface also limits oxygen from reaching the enzyme.

Browning is part of a plant's damage response. Similar reactions can make the injured tissue less inviting to some pests and microbes.

The brown color is a visible record of broken cells meeting the air, which means snack time lets you watch chemistry happen without a microscope.

The change feels almost like the apple is developing a bruise in fast-forward, except the trigger is the clean cut that opened its cells.
