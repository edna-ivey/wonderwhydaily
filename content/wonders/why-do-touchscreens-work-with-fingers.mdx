A phone screen looks like plain glass, but the moment your finger arrives it notices exactly where you touched.

Most phone screens use a transparent conductive grid. Your finger conducts electricity and changes the grid's local electric field, allowing software to calculate where the touch happened.

The screen checks the grid many times each second and can track several touches at once. That is how it follows a swipe, notices a pinch, and separates a deliberate gesture from a quick tap. Your gesture becomes a changing electrical map that software can interpret almost instantly as your fingers move.

Ordinary gloves block that electrical interaction. Touchscreen gloves weave conductive material into the fingertips so the screen can still detect the change.

Your finger is not pushing a hidden button. It is briefly becoming part of an invisible electrical map.

The glass can sense several fingers at once, letting two simple touches become a pinch, twist, swipe, or zoom.
