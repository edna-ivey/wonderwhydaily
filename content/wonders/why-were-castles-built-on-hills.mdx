A castle on a hill was part fortress, part lookout, and part enormous stone announcement.

High ground gave defenders a wider view and forced attackers to climb while carrying equipment. The slope made an approach slower and more tiring, while gravity favored people defending from above.

Height also made a castle visible from far away. Beyond defense, a fortress on the skyline announced control of the surrounding land to travelers, nearby communities, and anyone considering an attack. The view was part warning, part advertisement, and part watchtower.

Not every castle used a natural hill. Builders also raised large earth mounds, called mottes, so they could create a defensive height where the landscape did not provide one.

Height did not merely make attack harder. It made power visible from miles away.

Anyone approaching had to spend energy climbing while defenders could watch the whole journey from above.

Even before a battle began, the landscape had already chosen a side.
