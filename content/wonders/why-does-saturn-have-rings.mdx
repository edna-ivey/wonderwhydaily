Saturn's rings look like a single polished disk, but that smooth band is an illusion made from an unimaginable crowd.

Saturn's rings are countless separate pieces of mostly water ice orbiting the planet. They may include material from shattered moons and collisions, all spread into a wide, bright disk by Saturn's gravity.

The ring particles stay spread out because they orbit at different speeds. Pieces closer to Saturn travel faster than pieces farther away, while countless small collisions keep the disk broad and flat. Gravity turns a swarm of debris into an orderly-looking system.

The rings are enormous across but surprisingly thin. In many places, their thickness is measured in tens of meters, making them more like a vast sheet than a chunky doughnut.

From far away, billions of separate travelers become one bright shape, like a cosmic highway seen from impossibly high above.

Each piece follows its own orbit, yet from Earth the whole swarm looks calm enough to have been drawn with a compass.
