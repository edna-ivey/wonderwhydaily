A mountain is caught in a very slow contest. Forces inside Earth push rock upward while rain, ice, rivers, wind, and gravity work to tear it down.

Many great mountain ranges form where tectonic plates meet. When continents collide, neither plate easily sinks, so layers of crust crumple, fold, fault, and thicken. Rock that once sat near sea level can rise kilometers into the sky. Other mountains grow where one plate dives beneath another and feeds volcanoes, or where faults lift giant blocks of crust.

Height does not increase forever. As mountains rise, erosion attacks more exposed rock. Glaciers carve valleys, rivers carry sediment away, and landslides pull slopes downward. The mountain's own weight also presses on the softer material beneath it.

Different rocks and climates change the result. Hard rock may resist erosion, while heavy rain or repeated freezing can rapidly reshape a peak.

Mountains seem permanent because their contest unfolds far more slowly than a human lifetime. Yet every ridge records both construction and destruction. A tall peak is not simply a pile that reached upward; it is a place where Earth's building forces are still winning, at least for now.
