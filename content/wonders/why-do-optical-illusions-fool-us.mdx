An optical illusion is a friendly trap set for a brain that is usually extremely good at seeing.

Your brain interprets visual signals using context and fast shortcuts. Illusions arrange lines, shadows, colors, or motion cues so a normally helpful guess produces a surprising result.

Your brain is trying to infer a three-dimensional world from a flat image on each retina. Clues such as shadow and perspective usually help, but an illusion can make those clues quietly disagree. Perception is an interpretation, not a camera feed. The illusion exposes the guesswork.

Being fooled does not mean your vision is badly designed. The same shortcuts let you recognize objects and judge a complicated scene quickly without measuring every detail.

The moment an illusion fools you is the moment you catch your brain making one of its normally brilliant guesses out loud.

Even after you learn the trick, many illusions keep working. Knowing the answer does not always switch off the brain's first impression.
