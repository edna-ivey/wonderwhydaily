A pink lake can look as though someone poured strawberry milk across the landscape. The color is real, but it usually comes from living things far too small to see individually.

Many pink lakes are extremely salty. Those harsh conditions are difficult for fish and many ordinary freshwater organisms, but certain algae and microbes thrive there. Some produce red, orange, or pink pigments that protect their cells from intense sunlight or help them capture energy.

When enormous numbers of these organisms fill the water, their tiny patches of color blend into one vast rosy surface. Different lakes can be colored by different communities, including an alga called Dunaliella salina and salt-loving microbes known as haloarchaea.

The shade is not always permanent. Rainfall, evaporation, salt concentration, temperature, and the size of the microbial population can shift a lake from pale blush to deep red or back toward ordinary blue-gray water.

A pink lake is not simply water with an unusual mineral tint. It can be a giant visible signal from an invisible ecosystem. Seen from the shore or even from space, countless microscopic survivors turn an extreme environment into one of Earth's boldest natural color experiments.
