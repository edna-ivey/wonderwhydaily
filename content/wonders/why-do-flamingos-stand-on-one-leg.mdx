A flamingo standing on one leg looks as if it is performing a difficult yoga pose for hours. To the flamingo, it may be closer to leaning comfortably against an invisible chair.

Researchers have found that a flamingo's joints and body weight can line up over one leg in a very stable position. Once balanced, the bird may need surprisingly little muscle effort to remain upright. Its skeleton helps hold the pose instead of forcing the leg muscles to work constantly.

That makes switching legs a sensible way to rest, especially when the birds spend long periods standing in shallow water. Tucking one leg against the warm body may also reduce heat loss. Long bare legs provide plenty of surface area for warmth to escape, so keeping one out of cool water can help.

There may not be one single reason every flamingo chooses one leg at every moment. Comfort, temperature, and the bird's remarkable built-in balance can all contribute.

The pose looks unstable because a human body would wobble and tire. A flamingo's body tells a different story. Its one-legged silhouette is not a circus trick but an efficient resting position, shaped by joints that make standing still look wonderfully improbable.
