Drop a buttered slice of toast and gravity makes every decision. A falling cat is different. In a fraction of a second, it can figure out which way is down and rearrange its body in midair, almost like solving a puzzle while falling through it.

The trick is called the righting reflex. A cat's eyes and inner ears first help it sense its position. Then its unusually flexible spine lets the front and back halves of the body turn somewhat separately. By pulling some legs inward and stretching others outward, the cat changes how quickly each half rotates. One half twists one way, the other counters it, and the paws swing toward the ground without the cat needing to push against the air or a wall.

A cat is basically turning its body into two connected spinning shapes and adjusting them on the fly. Its tail can help with balance, but the flexible body is the real star of the maneuver.

Kittens begin developing this reflex when they are only a few weeks old. Still, it is not a superhero shield. Cats can be badly hurt by falls, and no height should ever be treated as safe. The wonder is not that cats ignore physics. It is that their bodies use physics with astonishing speed.
