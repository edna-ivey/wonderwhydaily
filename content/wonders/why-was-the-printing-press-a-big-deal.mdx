Before fast printing, making another copy of a book could mean another person writing the whole thing again by hand.

Movable type let printers arrange reusable letters, ink them, and make many consistent pages far faster than copying by hand. Books and pamphlets became cheaper and ideas could spread to many more readers.

Printers could produce matching copies that readers in different places could discuss and challenge. That made it easier for a scientific observation, political argument, or religious idea to become a shared public conversation. More people could respond to the very same words, creating faster debates.

Faster printing did not guarantee accurate information. It also made rumors, propaganda, and arguments easier to distribute, much like later communication technologies.

The press did more than make pages. It made it possible for the same idea to arrive in many minds before the idea had time to disappear.

A message could now travel farther than its author and remain consistent enough for distant readers to argue about the same words.
