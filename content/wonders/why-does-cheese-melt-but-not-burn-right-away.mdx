A slice of cheese can slump into a glossy puddle before dark spots appear, but not every cheese performs that trick. Some stretch, some leak oil, some hold their shape, and some begin browning before they melt smoothly.

Cheese is a mixture of water, fat, proteins, salt, and other compounds. As many cheeses warm, fat begins to liquefy and the protein network holding everything together loosens. That lets the cheese soften or flow.

Moisture changes the timing. Water in food cannot become much hotter than its boiling point while it remains liquid. As moisture absorbs heat and escapes as steam, it helps keep the cheese from racing immediately toward scorching temperatures. Once the surface becomes drier, it can grow hotter and begin browning. With enough heat and time, browned areas can char.

The exact result depends on the cheese. Moist mozzarella often melts and stretches. Aged cheeses may release oil or brown more quickly. Acid-set cheeses such as paneer can keep much of their shape because their proteins are connected differently.

Cheese therefore does not follow one universal melt-before-burn rule. Heat starts several changes at once, and the cheese's moisture and structure decide which transformation wins the race.
