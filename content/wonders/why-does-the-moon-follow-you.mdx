Ride in a car at night and everything nearby seems to hurry past: fences, trees, houses, signs. The Moon, meanwhile, calmly keeps its place beside you like the world's most patient travel companion.

It only looks that way because the Moon is extremely far away. When you move, nearby objects quickly change their position in your view. Pass a mailbox and it leaps from ahead of you to behind you in seconds. But the Moon is roughly 384,000 kilometers away. A short drive changes your viewing angle by such a tiny amount that your eyes cannot notice it. With no obvious sideways shift, your brain treats the Moon as if it is moving along with you.

This difference in apparent movement is called parallax. Distance turns it into a kind of visual speedometer: nearby things slide dramatically, while very distant things barely budge. Astronomers use the same idea on a gigantic scale, comparing views from different places to judge distances in space.

The Moon is not actually following your car. It is doing something far more impressive: racing around Earth at roughly one kilometer per second while appearing almost perfectly still. Its great distance hides that motion and turns an ordinary trip into a quiet lesson in perspective.
