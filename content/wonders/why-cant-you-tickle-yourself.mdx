You know exactly where your own fingers are going, and that ruins the trick. Tickling depends heavily on surprise.

Whenever you decide to move, your brain sends commands to your muscles. At the same time, it creates a prediction of what that movement should feel like. When your fingers reach your side, the expected touch arrives almost exactly on schedule. Your brain compares the prediction with the real sensation and turns down the response because there is little new information to investigate.

Think of it like receiving a package you packed yourself. You still feel its weight, but opening it does not produce much suspense.

Another person's hand is harder to forecast. You may know they plan to tickle you, but you cannot perfectly predict the exact timing, pressure, and path of every movement. That uncertainty keeps your nervous system alert and makes the sensation much stronger.

This prediction system does far more than spoil self-tickling. It helps you focus on unexpected events instead of being overwhelmed by sensations caused by every movement you make. Your brain is constantly labeling some experiences as “me” and turning down their volume.

You cannot easily surprise yourself with your own fingers because your brain has already read the next line of the story.
