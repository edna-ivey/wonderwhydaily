A strawberry looks like it ignored the normal fruit instructions and sprinkled all its seeds on the outside. The truth is stranger: those little specks are not technically naked seeds.

Each speck is a tiny dry fruit called an achene, and the actual seed sits inside it. The juicy red part we enjoy develops mostly from the flower's receptacle, the structure that once supported the flower's parts. As that tissue swells, it carries the many achenes across its surface.

That makes a strawberry an aggregate accessory fruit. “Aggregate” means it forms from many separate parts of one flower. “Accessory” means much of the fleshy part comes from tissue other than the flower's ovary.

Other fruits play their own botanical tricks. A raspberry is made from clusters of many small juicy units. A cashew's familiar nut grows beneath a swollen structure called a cashew apple. Everyday produce does not always match the simple fruit diagrams we imagine.

The next time you hold a strawberry, you are holding one enlarged flower base decorated with hundreds of individual fruits. It is less like one berry and more like an edible fruit neighborhood.

Botany has quietly hidden an entire crowd inside one familiar snack.
