Pineapple is one of the few snacks that seems to snack on you in return. After several juicy pieces, your tongue may feel prickly, tender, or strangely polished.

The main culprit is bromelain, a group of enzymes that breaks proteins into smaller pieces. Pineapple plants use these enzymes as part of their natural chemistry. When you eat fresh pineapple, bromelain briefly goes to work on proteins at the surface of your mouth. Your saliva and digestive system quickly handle the enzyme, and the cells lining your mouth repair themselves fast, so the sensation does not last.

Pineapple is also acidic. That acidity can make a sensitive mouth notice bromelain's work even more, especially after a large serving. Think of it as a two-person cleanup crew arriving when nobody asked for cleaning.

The odd feeling is strongest with fresh pineapple because heat changes the shape of enzymes and stops them from working properly. That is why canned or cooked pineapple often feels gentler. It is also why fresh pineapple can interfere with gelatin desserts: the active bromelain chops up the proteins that are supposed to hold the jelly together.

So the fruit is not attacking you, exactly. For a few minutes, you and your pineapple are simply digesting each other.
