A snapping shrimp carries a claw that behaves less like a fist and more like a tiny underwater special-effects machine.

When the shrimp closes its oversized claw, a small plunger-like part shoots into a matching pocket. That movement fires a jet of water so quickly that the pressure behind it suddenly drops. A bubble forms in the low-pressure region, but it does not last.

As the surrounding water crushes the bubble, the collapse releases a sharp shock wave and a loud snap. For an incredibly brief moment, the inside becomes hot and energetic enough to produce a faint flash of light. The light is called sonoluminescence. It is so quick and dim that you would need special equipment to notice it clearly.

The shock wave is the shrimp's useful weapon. It can stun small prey and defend the shrimp's hiding place without the claw needing to strike the target directly. The bubble does the hitting.

This is not the famous mantis shrimp's smashing punch; snapping shrimp use a different trick. One small animal moves water fast enough to create an empty pocket, then lets the ocean collapse that pocket into sound, force, and light. For a moment, a shrimp makes a star-like flash inside a bubble.
