Reading about yawning may already be making your jaw feel suspiciously ready to join in.

Yawning often appears when your brain is changing gears: waking up, getting sleepy, becoming bored, or shifting attention. The exact purpose is still debated, but it is not simply an emergency gulp of oxygen.

Because yawning appears during transitions, it may help the body shift its level of alertness. Scientists are still sorting out the details, which makes an ordinary yawn a surprisingly unfinished research question.

Yawns can be contagious after you see, hear, or even read about one. That effect shows how strongly attention and social cues can trigger a body action before you consciously decide to do it.

A yawn is an ordinary action with an unfinished scientific mystery inside it, which is a good reminder that familiar does not mean fully understood.

A yawn can spread through a room without anyone intending it, turning one person's body action into an oddly contagious suggestion.
