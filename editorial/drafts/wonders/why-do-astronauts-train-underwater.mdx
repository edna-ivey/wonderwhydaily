Practicing a spacewalk on Earth creates an obvious problem: Earth refuses to turn off gravity. One useful workaround is to send astronauts into an enormous swimming pool and carefully adjust their equipment until they neither sink quickly nor float quickly upward.

This condition is called neutral buoyancy. Divers add weights and flotation to an astronaut's training suit so the astronaut can hover near underwater mockups of spacecraft equipment. The water supports the body from many directions, making it possible to rehearse slow movements, tool use, repairs, and emergency procedures without standing on the floor.

Underwater training is not exactly like space. Water creates drag, so every movement pushes against resistance. In orbit, an astronaut and a heavy tool can keep drifting after a small shove. A training suit underwater also behaves differently from a real pressurized suit in a vacuum.

Even with those differences, the pool is a valuable rehearsal room. Teams can watch from nearby, repeat difficult steps, and discover where a handrail or tool might be hard to reach. By the time astronauts perform the real task hundreds of kilometers above Earth, they have already practiced the choreography.

The pool does not create space on Earth. It creates a place where spacewalk mistakes can be found before they matter.
