A website can feel as if it remembers you. Your shopping cart is still full. The language setting stays the same. You remain signed in while moving from one page to another. That memory often begins with a tiny browser note called a cookie.

The web is naturally forgetful. When a browser requests a page, the server sends a response, but the next request can look like a completely new visit. A cookie gives the browser a small piece of information to save and send back when appropriate. That information might identify a login session, remember a preference, or connect several page visits to the same cart.

Cookies are not little programs, and they cannot crawl around a computer eating files. They are stored data with rules controlling which website can receive them and when. Still, cookies can also be used to measure behavior or help track people across visits, which is why browsers provide privacy controls and websites often display consent choices.

Not every remembered detail uses a cookie, and deleting cookies may sign you out or reset preferences. The important idea is simple: a cookie acts like a claim ticket. The website does not have to know everything about you. It only needs enough information to recognize the right saved experience.
