A beach can seem to breathe. Water creeps up the sand, slips away, and returns hours later. The Moon sets the main rhythm, but real tides are more complicated than two neat piles of water circling Earth.

The Moon's gravity pulls on the entire planet. The pull is slightly stronger on the side closer to the Moon and slightly weaker on the far side. Those differences stretch the oceans and help create a repeating tidal pattern as Earth and the Moon move.

If Earth were covered by one deep, even ocean, that pattern would be simpler. Instead, continents block the water. Ocean basins have different shapes and depths. Coastlines, bays, seafloors, and the turning Earth redirect the moving water. That is why many places experience two high tides and two low tides during a lunar day, while some places have one strong high tide or two tides of very different heights.

The Sun also matters. When the Sun, Moon, and Earth line up, their effects combine to produce larger tidal ranges. When the Sun and Moon pull from different directions, the difference between high and low tide is usually smaller.

So the ocean is not simply following the Moon overhead. The Moon starts a planet-sized rhythm, and every coastline plays its own version.
